From the first POISON PITCH audit (cards 036–119, 2026-07-19). Three recurring failure modes. If these appear again in later POISON batches, escalate as entrenched taste debt, not one-off misses.

1. **The E3 "annoying-friend-habit" middle tier drifts GENERIC.** The wildly-absurdist E1 curses and the table-specific E4/E5 confessions are strong; the E3 tier ("you're the friend who X") is the weak middle because "annoying friend / roommate pet peeve" is the most well-trodden party-game ground. The default offenders: early-vs-late, fish-reheat/nail-clip, grammar-corrector/mispronouncer, first-impressions. These pass no swap test.

2. **The E4/E5 reveal/mirror template causes formula fatigue.** A large share of table-aware cards use the exact grammar "A: reveal X about them || B: Or learn/hear X about you." Individually on-voice; in bulk (~17 in one batch) it reads as a template and the deck feels same-y in play. Vary the B-side mechanic (make them fight back, sell it as a virtue, room guesses the target, read their face) instead of always mirroring.

3. **Duplicate / near-duplicate pairs.** Writers re-hit the same premise: early/late, borrowed-and-kept, invite-from-obligation, funeral-laugh, resentful-planner, "who'd miss you least." A duplicate is generic-by-repetition even when the copy is good. Dedup pass needed before ship.

**Why:** the taste law (`HELLDECK/CLAUDE.md`) bans statistical-average/AI-default output and demands each card pass the swap test. These three modes are exactly where a large hand-written batch quietly regresses to the mean.

**How to apply:** In future POISON audits, check the E3 tier hardest, count how many E4/E5 cards use the mirror template, and run a premise-dedup pass. See [[poison-card-quality-bar]].
