## The Complete Party Game Collection

_Low Cognitive Load. High Social Stakes. Maximum Chaos._

# 1\. Roast Consensus üéØ

### How to Play:

1.  **The Prompt:** A roast card appears (e.g., _"Who would eat mayo straight from the jar?"_).
2.  **The Vote:** Everyone secretly picks one victim.
3.  **The Countdown:** 20 seconds to lock in your choice.
4.  **The Reveal:** All votes drop simultaneously.
5.  **The Roast:** Whoever got the most votes takes the heat.

### The Mechanics:

-   **Simultaneous Reveal:** No one sees votes until the timer hits zero. No bandwagoning.
-   **Majority Rules:** The person with the most votes is crowned the Mayo Goblin (or whatever the prompt was).
-   **Ties:** Rock-paper-scissors. Loser takes the roast.

### Scoring:

-   **Majority Pick:** +2 Points (you voted with the hive mind).
-   **Room Heat Bonus:** If 80%+ of the room agrees on one person, everyone who voted for them gets +1 bonus point.
-   **The Roasted:** No points. Just shame.

### The Vibe:

-   **Instant Judgment:** This is _Survivor_ tribal council but faster and meaner.
-   **No Mercy:** If you get picked, own it. Denying it makes it worse.
-   **The Quiet Killer:** Sometimes the person who _doesn't_ get votes is the most suspicious.

### Tips:

-   Vote fast. Overthinking kills the vibe.
-   Consider who would _actually_ do this, not who you want to roast.
-   If you get picked, lean into it. "Yeah, I would. What about it?"

# 2\. Confession or Cap ü§•

### How to Play:

1.  **The Confessor:** Someone is chosen and receives a potentially embarrassing prompt (e.g., _"Have you ever stalked an ex on social media for over an hour?"_).
2.  **The Claim:** The Confessor answers **TRUE** or **FALSE**.
3.  **The Jury:** Everyone else votes on whether they believe the Confessor.
4.  **The Truth:** The answer is revealed.

### The Twist:

-   **Confessor Wins:** If they fool the majority (everyone guessed wrong), they get +2 points.
-   **Voters Win:** If you correctly called the lie (or truth), you get +1 point.
-   **Room Heat Bonus:** If the _entire_ room agrees and gets it right, everyone gets +1 bonus.

### Card Archetypes:

-   **The "Everyone Has Done This":** Prompts so common that lying is pointless (_"Have you ever peed in a pool?"_).
-   **The "Oddly Specific":** Weird enough that it could be true (_"Have you ever cried during a car commercial?"_).
-   **The "Career Ender":** Dark confessions that make you question your friendships (_"Have you ever lied on a resume?"_).
-   **The "Humble Brag":** Confessions that sound bad but are actually flexes (_"Have you ever been kicked out of a country?"_).

### The Vibe:

-   **Poker Face Required:** The best liars don't flinch. The worst liars laugh immediately.
-   **Trust Issues:** After three rounds, you will never believe your friends again.
-   **The Tell:** Watch for nervous laughter, long pauses, or over-explaining. Liars talk too much.

### Scoring:

-   **Confessor (Successful Lie):** +2 Points.
-   **Voters (Correct Guess):** +1 Point.
-   **Room Heat (100% Agreement + Correct):** +1 Bonus to all voters.

### Tips:

-   **Sell the truth like a lie.** If it's true, act suspicious. People will think you're bluffing.
-   **Sell the lie like the truth.** Confidence is everything. Say it fast and move on.
-   **Study the person.** If they're a chronic oversharer, a "FALSE" answer is probably a lie.

# 3\. Poison Pitch üíÄ

### How to Play:

1.  **The Dilemma:** A "Would You Rather" card is drawn with two horrifying options (e.g., _"Would you rather fight one horse-sized duck OR 100 duck-sized horses?"_).
2.  **The Assignment:** One person is randomly assigned to defend **Option A**. Another person defends **Option B**.
3.  **The Pitch:** Each pitcher has 30 seconds to argue why their option is the _better_ choice.
4.  **The Vote:** The group votes for the most convincing argument (not the better option‚Äîthe better _pitch_).

### The Rules:

-   **You Don't Choose Your Side:** The game assigns you the option. You might have to defend the indefensible.
-   **Humor Beats Logic:** A hilarious argument for a terrible option will beat a boring argument for a good option.
-   **No Cop-Outs:** You cannot say "both options suck." You must sell your poison like it's medicine.

### Card Types:

-   **The Physical Nightmare:** Painful, disgusting, or dangerous scenarios (_"Eat a live spider OR lick a subway pole"_).
-   **The Social Suicide:** Reputation-destroying choices (_"Drunk text your boss OR your ex"_).
-   **The Moral Dilemma:** Ethically questionable options (_"Steal from a charity OR lie under oath"_).
-   **The Absurd Hypothetical:** Scenarios so ridiculous that logic doesn't apply (_"Fight a bear with a spoon OR seduce a bear with poetry"_).

### The Vibe:

-   **Debate Club on Acid:** You are a lawyer defending a guilty client. Make it work.
-   **Embrace the Absurd:** The worse your option is, the funnier your pitch can be.
-   **Confidence is Key:** If you believe it, they'll believe it.

### Scoring:

-   **Winning Pitcher:** +2 Points (your argument won, regardless of how bad your option was).
-   **Losing Pitcher:** Nothing. You tried.

### Tips:

-   **Reframe the horror.** "Sure, 100 duck-sized horses sounds bad, but think of the _cuteness factor_."
-   **Attack the other option.** "You want to fight ONE horse-sized duck? That thing will _murder_ you."
-   **Use personal attacks.** "You think _you_ could handle a horse-sized duck? You can't even open a pickle jar."

# 4\. Fill-In Finisher ‚úçÔ∏è

### How to Play:

1.  **The Judge:** Someone is the Judge. They draw a card with a two-part prompt (e.g., _"I got kicked out of Disney World for \_\_\_\_\_, and now I'm banned from \_\_\_\_\_."_).
2.  **The Setup:** The Judge reads the card aloud and fills in the **first blank** verbally (e.g., _"I got kicked out of Disney World for **punching Goofy**..."_).
3.  **The Punchline:** Everyone else has **60 seconds** to write down their answer for the **second blank** (e.g., _"...and now I'm banned from **all theme parks in North America**."_).
4.  **The Reading:** The Judge reads all anonymous responses aloud.
5.  **The Winner:** The Judge picks their favorite. That person wins the round.

### The Strategy:

-   **Write for the Judge:** If the Judge loves dark humor, go dark. If they love puns, go punny.
-   **Short and Punchy:** Long answers lose momentum. Keep it tight.
-   **Callback Humor:** Reference something that happened earlier in the game or an inside joke.

### Card Archetypes:

-   **The Crime Scene:** _"I got arrested for \_\_\_\_\_, and the cop said \_\_\_\_\_."_
-   **The Breakup:** _"I broke up with my ex because of \_\_\_\_\_, and now they're dating \_\_\_\_\_."_
-   **The Job Interview:** _"I didn't get the job because I mentioned \_\_\_\_\_, and the interviewer said \_\_\_\_\_."_
-   **The Family Dinner:** _"Thanksgiving was ruined when someone brought up \_\_\_\_\_, and Grandma said \_\_\_\_\_."_

### The Vibe:

-   **Cards Against Humanity Energy:** But with a twist‚Äîthe Judge controls half the joke, so you're riffing off _their_ setup.
-   **Improv Comedy:** You're the "Yes, and..." to the Judge's premise.
-   **Tailored Chaos:** Every round feels different because the Judge's personality shapes the humor.

### Scoring:

-   **Winner:** +1 Point.
-   **Judge Role:** Rotates clockwise after each round.

### Tips:

-   **Read the room.** If the Judge is conservative, don't write "cocaine."
-   **Subvert expectations.** If the setup is dark, try a wholesome punchline. If the setup is innocent, go nuclear.
-   **Timing matters.** The funniest answer is often the one that makes the Judge laugh _while_ they're reading it aloud.

# 5\. Red Flag Rally üö©

### How to Play:

1.  **The Scenario:** A dating card is revealed with a **Perk** and a **Red Flag** (e.g., _"They're a billionaire, BUT they collect toenail clippings."_).
2.  **The Defender:** Someone is randomly assigned to defend this person and argue why they're still dateable.
3.  **The Pitch:** The Defender has 45 seconds to make their case.
4.  **The Vote:** Everyone else votes **SMASH** (would date) or **PASS** (absolutely not).

### The Verdict:

-   **Defender Wins:** If the majority votes **SMASH**, the Defender gets +2 points.
-   **Defender Loses:** If the majority votes **PASS**, the Defender drinks/takes a penalty.

### Card Types:

-   **The Dealbreaker:** Red flags so horrifying that no perk can save it (_"They're a doctor, BUT they don't believe in washing hands."_).
-   **The Negotiable:** Red flags that are weird but manageable (_"They're hilarious, BUT they talk to their plants like children."_).
-   **The Trap:** The perk is so good that you _want_ to overlook the red flag (_"They own a private island, BUT they're in a cult."_).
-   **The Reverse:** The "perk" is questionable, and the "red flag" is actually worse (_"They're really into astrology, BUT they also think the Earth is flat."_).

### The Vibe:

-   **Desperate Dating Show:** You are the contestant on _The Bachelor_ trying to justify a terrible decision.
-   **Moral Flexibility:** How much are you willing to overlook for money, looks, or convenience?
-   **Group Roast:** Even if the Defender wins, the group will roast them for defending someone who "collects human teeth."

### Scoring:

-   **Defender (Majority SMASH):** +2 Points.
-   **Defender (Majority PASS):** Penalty/Drink.

### Tips:

-   **Downplay the red flag.** "Okay, yes, they collect toenails, but _everyone_ has a hobby."
-   **Emphasize the perk.** "They're a _billionaire_. You can afford therapy for the toenail thing."
-   **Use humor.** "Look, I've dated worse. At least they're _organized_ about their toenail collection."

# 6\. Hot Seat Imposter üé≠

### How to Play:

1.  **The Target:** Someone is selected as the "Target."
2.  **The Imposter:** Another person is secretly chosen to impersonate the Target.
3.  **The Questions:** The group asks 3-5 personal questions (e.g., _"What's your biggest fear?"_ or _"What's the last thing you Googled?"_).
4.  **The Performance:** The Imposter answers each question _as if they were the Target_, trying to sound convincing.
5.  **The Vote:** After all questions are answered, the group votes: **REAL** (they believe the Imposter) or **FAKE** (they see through the act).

### The Twist:

-   **The Target Stays Silent:** The real Target cannot speak during the questioning. They just watch the Imposter try to become them.
-   **Subtle Details Win:** The best Imposters don't exaggerate. They mimic speech patterns, inside jokes, and small quirks.

### Scoring:

-   **Imposter (Fools Majority):** +2 Points.
-   **Target (Group Sees Through It):** +1 Point.
-   **Voters (Correct Guess):** +1 Point each.

### Question Types:

-   **The Softball:** Easy questions that anyone could answer (_"What's your favorite food?"_).
-   **The Curveball:** Questions that require specific knowledge (_"What did you do last Tuesday?"_).
-   **The Trap:** Questions designed to expose the Imposter (_"What's the name of your childhood pet?"_).
-   **The Roast:** Questions that force the Imposter to insult the Target (_"What's your most embarrassing moment?"_).

### The Vibe:

-   **Identity Theft Simulator:** You are literally stealing someone's personality for 60 seconds.
-   **Paranoia:** The Target will never trust you again after you reveal their "secret fear of mayonnaise."
-   **Method Acting:** The best Imposters _become_ the Target. Posture, tone, even facial expressions matter.

### Tips:

-   **Study your friends.** Pay attention to how they talk, what they complain about, and their go-to stories.
-   **Commit fully.** If you hesitate, you're caught. Answer with confidence even if you're guessing.
-   **Use callbacks.** Reference something the Target mentioned earlier in the night to sell the illusion.

# 7\. Text Thread Trap üì±

### How to Play:

1.  **The Scenario:** A card is drawn displaying an awkward or high-stakes "received text message."
2.  **The Modifier:** A Mandatory Tone is generated (1-22).
3.  **The Reply:** The person must verbally improvise the text reply (or dictate it to the group) while strictly acting out that specific Tone.
4.  **The Vote:** The group votes on whether the person survived the social interaction.

### The Mandatory Tones:

1.  **The Seductive Whisper** (Breathless, trying to take them home)
2.  **The Raging Karen** (Demanding a manager, everything is unacceptable)
3.  **The 1920s News Anchor** (Fast-talking, high-pitched, formal)
4.  **The Malfunctioning Robot** (Monotone with glitches/beeps)
5.  **The Noir Detective** (Gritty internal monologue, cynical)
6.  **The Weeping Soap Star** (Hysterical sobbing, tragic)
7.  **The Aggressive Drill Sergeant** (Screaming, calling them maggots)
8.  **The Stoned Philosopher** (Slow, mind-blown, "Whoa dude")
9.  **The Paralyzingly Shy** (Stuttering, terrified, whispering)
10.  **The Used Car Salesman** (Sleazy, fast, high-pressure)
11.  **The Shakespearean Actor** (Over-enunciated, dramatic projection)
12.  **The Conspiracy Theorist** (Paranoid, whispering, "They are listening")
13.  **The Valley Girl** (Vocal fry, constant up-speak?)
14.  **The Medieval Peasant** (Downtrodden, scared of plague)
15.  **The Bond Villain** (Calm, slow, expecting them to die)
16.  **The Manic Squirrel** (2x speed, twitchy, wide-eyed)
17.  **The Dying Breath** (Gasping, 5 seconds to live)
18.  **The Condescending IT Guy** (Sighing loudly, smarter than you)
19.  **The Possessed** (Guttural growls, tongues)
20.  **The Confused Time Traveler** (From 1850, confused by tech)
21.  **The Golf Commentator** (Hushed whisper, respectful, boring)
22.  **The Frantic 911 Caller** (Emergency panic)

### Rules for Cards:

-   **Context:** Cards must simulate a notification popping up on a lock screen.
-   **Brevity:** The prompt must be short (1-2 sentences max) to allow for immediate reaction.
-   **Ambiguity:** The text should be open-ended enough that any Tone _could_ work, but most will be disastrous.
-   **Stakes:** Low stakes (grandma) or maximum stakes (FBI) only. No middle ground.

### Card Types:

-   **The "We Need To Talk":** Relationship terrors, exes reaching out, or "who is this girl" texts.
-   **The Corporate HR:** Bosses asking for quick calls, unexpected firing rumors, or accidental "reply all" disasters.
-   **The Family Tech Support:** Boomer parents asking how to delete the internet or posting inappropriate statuses.
-   **The Wrong Number:** Bizarre requests from strangers (drug deals, cryptic warnings) that you must handle.
-   **The Group Chat:** A chaotic thread where you are being roasted or asked to pick a side.

### The Vibe:

-   **Panic:** The feeling of staring at those three typing dots (...) for too long.
-   **Social Anxiety:** The game thrives on the discomfort of mismatched tones (e.g., using "Seductive Whisper" to reply to "Grandma is in the hospital").
-   **Fast Paced:** No time to think. Read, React, Send.

### Scoring:

-   **Success (Majority Vote):** +2 Points.
-   **Failure (Breaking Character):** -1 Point.
-   **Room Heat Bonus:** Perfect improvisation that makes the entire room lose it = +1 bonus.

### Tips:

-   **Consider context carefully, then ignore it if the Tone demands it.**
-   **Physicality helps.** Hold an imaginary phone, use facial expressions, commit to the bit.
-   **Sometimes the best reply is a voicemail** (if the Tone allows).

# 8\. Taboo Timer ‚è±Ô∏è

### How to Play:

1.  **The Guesser & The Clue-Giver:** Everyone splits into two roles. One person is the Clue-Giver, the rest are Guessers.
2.  **The Target Word:** The Clue-Giver draws a card with a target word (e.g., _"WEDDING"_).
3.  **The Forbidden Words:** The card lists 3-5 words the Clue-Giver **cannot say** (e.g., _"Bride, Groom, Ceremony, Ring, Dress"_).
4.  **The Clock:** 60 seconds on the timer. The Clue-Giver must describe the target word without using any forbidden words.
5.  **The Guess:** Guessers shout out answers. If someone guesses correctly, the Clue-Giver draws a new card and keeps going.

### The Penalty:

-   **Forbidden Word Spoken:** If the Clue-Giver accidentally says a forbidden word, the card is discarded and they lose 1 point.
-   **Buzzer Role:** One person acts as the "Buzzer" and listens carefully for slip-ups.

### Card Archetypes:

-   **The Obvious:** Words everyone knows, but the forbidden words make it nearly impossible (_"PIZZA" - Forbidden: Cheese, Italy, Slice, Delivery, Pepperoni_).
-   **The Abstract:** Concepts that are hard to describe (_"LOVE" - Forbidden: Heart, Feeling, Romance, Relationship, Emotion_).
-   **The Pop Culture:** References that require creative clues (_"HARRY POTTER" - Forbidden: Wizard, Magic, Hogwarts, Wand, Book_).
-   **The Oddly Specific:** Niche words that require lateral thinking (_"SPATULA" - Forbidden: Kitchen, Flip, Cook, Pancake, Utensil_).

### The Vibe:

-   **Pressure Cooker:** The timer is ticking, your brain is melting, and you just said "BRIDE" when describing a wedding.
-   **Synonym Hell:** You will learn how limited your vocabulary actually is.
-   **Team Chaos:** Guessers will shout the _forbidden words_ constantly, making the Clue-Giver paranoid.

### Scoring:

-   **+2 per successful guess** (within the 60-second window).
-   **\-1 for each forbidden word spoken.**
-   **Bonus:** If the team guesses 5+ words in one round, everyone gets +1 bonus point.

### Tips:

-   **Think of synonyms quickly.** "Wedding" becomes "the thing where two people sign a legal contract in fancy clothes."
-   **Use examples and analogies.** "It's like a birthday party, but someone cries and it costs $30,000."
-   **Stay calm under pressure.** Panicking makes you say the forbidden words faster.

# 9\. The Unifying Theory üìê

### How to Play:

1.  **The Trio:** A card is revealed displaying three completely unrelated items (e.g., _"A Catholic Priest, A Referee, A Zebra"_).
2.  **The Challenge:** The person must explain exactly why these three things are **The Same**.
3.  **The Logic:** You must find the single thread‚Äîno matter how thin, absurd, or dark‚Äîthat connects all three.
4.  **The Delivery:** Present your "Unifying Theory" to the group.
5.  **The Vote:** The group decides if your theory holds water or if you're just rambling.

### The "Spice Level" Clause:

-   **Spice 1-3:** Connections should be clever, literal, or silly.
-   **Spice 4+ (The Danger Zone):** If the game is set to Spice Level 4 or higher, the connection **must** be inappropriate, sexual, politically incorrect, or morally bankrupt.
-   _Example (Spice 1):_ Priest/Ref/Zebra ‚Üí "They all wear black and white."
-   _Example (Spice 5):_ Priest/Ref/Zebra ‚Üí "If you see one running at you in the wild, you are about to be screwed."

### Card Archetypes:

-   **The "Visual Gag":** Three things that look vaguely similar but serve different purposes.
    -   _Ex:_ A Cucumber, The Eiffel Tower, A Sword.
-   **The "Social Hazard":** Three people/groups you never want to meet.
    -   _Ex:_ Your Ex, The IRS, A Magician.
-   **The "Impossible Reach":** Three things so distinct it seems impossible.
    -   _Ex:_ The Moon, A Ham Sandwich, 9/11.
-   **The "Setup":** Two innocent things and one terrible thing, forcing you to ruin the innocent things to make the connection work.

### The Vibe:

-   **Conspiracy Theorist:** You are the guy with the red string on the corkboard. _Everything is connected._
-   **Desperate Lawyer:** You know you are lying, but you have to sell it to the jury to survive.
-   **Corrupt Philosopher:** Finding deep, dark meanings in everyday objects.

### Scoring:

-   **+2 for the most convincing or hilarious Theory.**
-   **\-1 for stating a fact that only applies to two items** ("Two of them are alive"). It must apply to **all three**.

### Tips:

-   **Look for Verbs:** Focus on what the items _do_ (or what is done _to_ them) rather than what they _are_.
-   **Go Dark:** When in doubt, "They all eventually die" or "They can all be used as a weapon" are safe bets.
-   **Commit:** If you pause, the theory falls apart. Speed sells the lie.

# 10\. Title Fight ü•ä

### How to Play:

1.  **The Ambush:** You draw a card. Immediately point at someone else and yell **"FIGHT!"**
2.  **The Challenge:** Read the challenge on the card aloud (e.g., _"Category: Pizza Toppings"_).
3.  **The Clash:** You and that person compete instantly. No countdowns, no warm-ups.
4.  **The End:** The first person to mess up, pause, or quit is the Loser.

### The 3 Ways to Fight:

#### **The Brain (Categories):**

-   **Action:** Ping-pong answers back and forth rapidly. The card sets the topic.
-   **Losing Condition:** If you repeat an answer, stutter, or pause for more than 3 seconds, you lose.
-   **Example:** _"Names of Harry Potter characters."_ (You ‚Üí Them ‚Üí You ‚Üí Them...)

#### **The Body (Speed):**

-   **Action:** A physical race to complete a simple task.
-   **Losing Condition:** Being second place.
-   **Example:** _"First person to touch a doorknob wins."_

#### **The Soul (Guts):**

-   **Action:** A test of willpower or awkwardness.
-   **Losing Condition:** Breaking eye contact, laughing, or flinching.
-   **Example:** _"Staring contest. You cannot blink or smile."_

### Scoring:

-   **Winner:** +1 Point (and bragging rights).
-   **Loser:** -1 Point (and takes the penalty/drink).

### Tips:

-   **Pick Your Victim Wisely:** If the card is a staring contest, don't pick the person with the glassy, unblinking eyes.
-   **Be Loud:** If you shout your answer with confidence, people might not notice it was wrong.
-   **Referees:** The people not fighting are the judges. If they say you paused too long, you lose. No arguments.

# 11\. Alibi Drop üïµÔ∏è

### How to Play:

1.  **The Crime:** Draw a card that accuses you of a specific scenario (e.g., _"Why is your search history just 'how to hide a body'?"_).
2.  **The Evidence:** The card lists **3 Mandatory Words** (e.g., _Pineapple, Tinder, Spatula_).
3.  **The Alibi:** You have 30 seconds to explain yourself. You must weave all three words into your story naturally.
4.  **The Verdict:** After you finish, the group acts as the Jury.

-   **Phase 1 (Believability):** Was the story convincing? If it was nonsense, you are **Guilty**.
-   **Phase 2 (The Catch):** The group tries to guess your 3 Mandatory Words. If they correctly identify the words you hid, you are **Guilty**.

### To Win:

You must tell a smooth story _AND_ fool the group so they can't tell which words were forced.

### Card Examples:

-   **The Suspicious Package:** _"What is in this bag you're trying to sneak onto the plane?"_
    -   _Hidden Words:_ A hamster, Glitter, The Declaration of Independence.
-   **The Missing Person:** _"Where were you the night your boss disappeared?"_
    -   _Hidden Words:_ Zumba Class, A Chainsaw, Gluten-free Bread.

### Scoring:

-   **Innocent (Success):** +2 Points. (Story was good AND the group failed to guess your words).
-   **Guilty (Failure):** -1 Point/Penalty. (The story made no sense OR the group caught your words).

### Tips:

-   **Don't Emphasize:** If you say "And then I picked up a... uh... _SPATULA_," you will get caught immediately. Say it casually.
-   **Use Decoys:** Throw in other random, weird nouns (that aren't on the card) to confuse the Jury.
-   **Speed Kills:** Speaking faster makes it harder for the Jury to flag specific words.

# 12\. Reality Check ü™û

### The Concept:

A brutal game of self-awareness. Someone rates themselves on a specific trait, and the group rates them on the same trait. If the numbers don't match, you are either **Delusional** or **Fishing**.

### How to Play:

1.  **The Question:** The active person (The Subject) draws a card with a specific trait (e.g., _"How funny are you, really?"_).
2.  **The Ego Score:** The Subject _secretly_ writes down a rating from **1 to 10** based on how they view themselves.
3.  **The Reality Score:** Simultaneously, the rest of the group discusses aloud and agrees on a single rating (1-10) for The Subject.
4.  **The Reveal:** Both numbers are revealed at the same time.

### The Verdict (Scoring):

-   **Self-Aware (Gap is 0-1):** The scores match! You know exactly who you are. **(+2 Points)**.
-   **Delusional (Ego is Higher):** You think you are a 9, but the group thinks you are a 4. You are living in a fantasy world. **(The Group Roasts You + You Drink)**.
-   **The Fisher (Ego is Lower):** You wrote a 4, but the group thinks you are an 8. You are obviously just fishing for compliments. We hate that. **(The Group Boos You + You Drink)**.

### Card Examples:

-   **The Fighter:** _"Rating: Your ability to win a bar fight against a random stranger."_
-   **The Lover:** _"Rating: Your performance in the bedroom."_
-   **The Survivor:** _"Rating: How long you would last in a zombie apocalypse before crying."_
-   **The Liar:** _"Rating: How convincing you are when you lie to your parents."_

### The Vibe:

-   **No Pity:** If someone underrates themselves ("The Fisher"), do not hug them. Accuse them of being a "Pick-Me" who just wanted to hear nice things.
-   **Loud Debate:** The group _must_ discuss the rating in front of the Subject (_"He thinks he's a 7? No way, remember the time he tripped over his own cat? He's a 3."_).
-   **The "Perfect 10":** If someone rates themselves a 10 and the group rates them a 1, they must finish their drink immediately. This is a **Critical Delusion**.

# 13\. Scatterblast üí£

### The Concept:

A high-speed category elimination game. The phone acts as a bomb with a hidden timer. The "Turn" moves around the circle, but the phone stays put.

### How to Play:

1.  **The Setup:** Place the phone in the center of the table (or assign a "Host" to hold it).
2.  **The Trigger:** A card reveals a **Category** + **Letter** (e.g., _"Celebrities" + "J"_).
3.  **The Fuse:** The timer starts (invisible duration: 20s - 60s).
4.  **The Relay:**

-   Player 1 shouts a valid answer (_"Jennifer Aniston!"_).
-   The Host (or Player 1) taps the **"Next"** button on the screen.
-   The "Turn" instantly passes to the person on the left.
-   Player 2 must shout a new answer (_"Jack Black!"_) and the button is tapped again.

5.  **The Explosion:** If the bomb sound plays while it is _your_ turn to speak, you lose.

### The "Challenge" Rule:

If someone shouts a nonsense answer or repeats a word, the group yells **"LOCKED!"** The Host _does not_ tap the button. The current person keeps the turn and must come up with a _new_ answer while the timer burns down.

### Card Variations:

-   **Standard:** Category + Start Letter.
-   **The Chain:** Last letter = First letter. (Apple ‚Üí Elephant ‚Üí Tiger).
-   **No Repeats:** Just the Category (no letter constraint), but you cannot repeat an answer ever used in previous rounds.

### The Vibe:

-   **Spectator Sport:** Since you aren't physically fumbling with a phone, you can focus entirely on staring at the victim and counting the seconds.

# 14\. Over / Under üìâ

### The Concept:

1.  **The Subject:** Someone is chosen (The Subject).
2.  **The Stat:** A card is drawn asking for a number regarding The Subject (e.g., _"Total number of photos in their Camera Roll"_).
3.  **The Line:** The group briefly discusses and agrees on a "Line" (e.g., _"We think he's vain, but not crazy... let's set the line at 1,500"_).
4.  **The Bet:** Everyone (except The Subject) votes:

-   **OVER üîº** (The real number is higher).
-   **UNDER üîΩ** (The real number is lower).

5.  **The Receipt:** The Subject _immediately_ checks their phone or answers truthfully to reveal the exact number.

### The Payoff:

-   **Winners:** Those who bet correctly get +1 Point.
-   **Losers:** Those who bet wrong take a Penalty/Drink.
-   **Exact Match:** If the number is _exactly_ the Line, everyone drinks except The Subject (who is essentially a god).

# AI CARD GENERATION MASTER GUIDE

## Introduction: The Science of Social Friction

This section is the blueprint for generating cards that create **maximum social chaos with minimum cognitive load**. Every card must pass the **"Drunk Person Test"**: Can someone who's three drinks in understand this immediately and want to play?

The secret to addictive party games isn't complexity‚Äîit's **emotional leverage**. Cards must tap into:

-   **Ego** (How do I look to others?)
-   **Status** (Who's winning the social hierarchy?)
-   **Secrets** (What are we hiding?)
-   **Judgment** (Who's getting roasted?)

### The Universal Card Principles:

1.  **Brevity is King:** If a card requires more than 10 seconds to read, it's too long.
2.  **Ambiguity Creates Chaos:** The best cards have multiple valid interpretations.
3.  **Stakes Must Be Clear:** People need to know immediately what they're risking (reputation, points, dignity).
4.  **Relatability Wins:** Inside jokes are great, but universal human experiences are better.
5.  **The Roast Must Land:** Cards should make people laugh _at_ someone, not feel genuinely hurt.

## Game 3: Poison Pitch - Card Generation Guide

### What the AI is Generating:

**"Would You Rather" Scenarios** - Two equally terrible options that force people to defend the indefensible.

### The Formula:

### The Psychology:

This game works because of **cognitive dissonance**. Both options are bad, so the humor comes from watching someone try to rationalize their choice. The best cards make both options so horrifying that there's no "right" answer.

Painful, disgusting, or dangerous scenarios.

**Why it works:** These are visceral. People can _feel_ the horror of each option.

Reputation-destroying choices that affect how others see you.

-   _"Would you rather drunk text your boss OR your ex?"_
-   _"Would you rather accidentally send a nude to your family group chat OR your work Slack?"_
-   _"Would you rather be known as 'the person who cries at work' OR 'the person who never washes their hands'?"_

**Why it works:** Social death is worse than physical pain. These cards attack your reputation.

#### **The Moral Dilemma:**

Ethically questionable choices that reveal your character.

-   _"Would you rather steal from a charity OR lie under oath?"_
-   _"Would you rather cheat to win $1 million OR play fair and lose?"_
-   _"Would you rather save your pet OR a stranger's child?"_

**Why it works:** These cards force people to reveal their values. The group will judge them either way.

#### **The Absurd Hypothetical:**

Scenarios so ridiculous that logic doesn't apply.

-   _"Would you rather fight a bear with a spoon OR seduce a bear with poetry?"_
-   _"Would you rather have fingers for toes OR toes for fingers?"_
-   _"Would you rather speak only in rhymes OR sing everything you say?"_

**Why it works:** The absurdity removes the pressure. People can be creative and silly.

#### **The "Forever" Curse:**

Permanent conditions that affect your entire life.

-   _"Would you rather never be able to use your phone again OR never be able to eat your favorite food again?"_
-   _"Would you rather always be 10 minutes late OR always be 20 minutes early?"_
-   _"Would you rather have to announce every time you fart OR have to ask permission every time you want to speak?"_

**Why it works:** The permanence raises the stakes. This isn't a one-time event‚Äîit's your new reality.

#### **The "Lose-Lose" Scenario:**

Both options are so bad that choosing feels impossible.

-   _"Would you rather have everyone you love forget you OR forget everyone you love?"_
-   _"Would you rather be able to read minds but never turn it off OR have everyone able to read your mind?"_
-   _"Would you rather live without music OR live without movies?"_

**Why it works:** These are genuinely difficult choices. The pitch becomes about damage control.

### Generation Rules:

-   **Balance the options.** If one option is clearly better, the game breaks. Both must be equally terrible.
-   **Make it visual.** "Fight a horse-sized duck" creates a mental image. "Experience discomfort" does not.
-   **Avoid trauma.** "Would you rather lose a parent or a sibling?" is too dark for a party game.
-   **Test for pitch-ability.** Can someone make a funny argument for either option? If not, rewrite.

### The "Spice Level" Modifier:

-   **Spice 1-2:** Silly, absurd scenarios (animal fights, weird superpowers).
-   **Spice 3-4:** Mildly embarrassing social scenarios (drunk texts, public mishaps).
-   **Spice 5+:** Dark moral dilemmas, sexual scenarios, or reputation-destroying choices.

### Bad Examples (and Why):

-   ‚ùå _"Would you rather be rich OR be happy?"_ (Too philosophical, not funny)
-   ‚ùå _"Would you rather die by fire OR die by drowning?"_ (Too dark, not fun)

## Game 13: Scatterblast - Card Generation Guide

### What the AI is Generating:

**Category + Letter Combinations** - A category (e.g., "Celebrities") and a starting letter (e.g., "J") that people must use to generate rapid-fire answers.

### The Formula:

`"CATEGORY: [Broad topic]"` `"LETTER: [Starting letter]"`

### The Psychology:

This game exploits **time pressure** and **mental blanking**. The best categories are:

1.  **Broad enough** that there are many possible answers.
2.  **Specific enough** that people can't just say random words.
3.  **Accessible enough** that everyone knows _some_ answers.

### Card Categories:

#### **The Pop Culture:**

Categories related to movies, TV, music, or celebrities.

-   _"CATEGORY: Celebrities / LETTER: J"_
-   _"CATEGORY: Disney Movies / LETTER: T"_
-   _"CATEGORY: Song Titles / LETTER: L"_

**Why it works:** Pop culture is universally accessible. Everyone knows _some_ celebrities or movies.

#### **The Geography:**

Categories related to places, countries, or cities.

-   _"CATEGORY: Countries / LETTER: S"_
-   _"CATEGORY: US States / LETTER: M"_
-   _"CATEGORY: Cities / LETTER: P"_

**Why it works:** Geography is taught in school, so most people know the basics. Under pressure, though, your brain blanks.

#### **The Food & Drink:**

Categories related to eating, drinking, or cooking.

-   _"CATEGORY: Pizza Toppings / LETTER: P"_
-   _"CATEGORY: Cocktails / LETTER: M"_
-   _"CATEGORY: Fast Food Chains / LETTER: B"_

**Why it works:** Food is relatable. Everyone eats, so everyone has answers.

#### **The Animals:**

Categories related to wildlife, pets, or creatures.

-   _"CATEGORY: Animals / LETTER: R"_
-   _"CATEGORY: Dog Breeds / LETTER: G"_
-   _"CATEGORY: Birds / LETTER: C"_

**Why it works:** Animals are easy to visualize, but under pressure, you'll forget every animal you've ever heard of.

#### **The Everyday Objects:**

Categories related to household items, tools, or common objects.

-   _"CATEGORY: Things in a Kitchen / LETTER: S"_
-   _"CATEGORY: Clothing Items / LETTER: J"_
-   _"CATEGORY: Tools / LETTER: H"_

**Why it works:** These are things people see every day, but naming them on the spot is harder than it sounds.

#### **The "Impossible" Letter:**

Categories paired with difficult letters (Q, X, Z).

-   _"CATEGORY: Countries / LETTER: Q"_ (Qatar, maybe?)
-   _"CATEGORY: Animals / LETTER: X"_ (X-ray fish? Xenops?)
-   _"CATEGORY: Foods / LETTER: Z"_ (Zucchini, ziti, and... uh...)

**Why it works:** These combinations are designed to make people struggle. The bomb will explode before they think of a third answer.

### Generation Rules:

-   **Avoid overly specific categories.** "Types of 18th-century French furniture" is too niche.
-   **Avoid overly broad categories.** "Things" is too vague.
-   **Test for answer density.** Can you name 10+ answers in this category with this letter? If not, it's too hard.
-   **Balance easy and hard letters.** "S" and "C" are easy. "Q" and "X" are hard.

### The "Chain" Variation:

Instead of a starting letter, the last letter of one answer becomes the first letter of the next.

-   _"CATEGORY: Animals"_
-   _"CHAIN: Dog ‚Üí Giraffe ‚Üí Elephant ‚Üí Tiger ‚Üí Raccoon..."_

**Why it works:** This adds an extra layer of difficulty. People must think ahead to avoid dead-end letters.

### Bad Examples (and Why):

-   ‚ùå _"CATEGORY: Things / LETTER: A"_ (Too vague, infinite answers)
-   ‚ùå _"CATEGORY: Nobel Prize Winners in Physics / LETTER: R"_ (Too niche, most people can't name one)
-   ‚ùå _"CATEGORY: Colors / LETTER: Q"_ (Impossible, no valid answers)

### Good Examples (and Why):

-   ‚úÖ _"CATEGORY: Countries / LETTER: C"_ (Accessible, many answers: Canada, China, Colombia, etc.)
-   ‚úÖ _"CATEGORY: Fruits / LETTER: B"_ (Easy but not too easy: Banana, Blueberry, Blackberry, etc.)
-   ‚úÖ _"CATEGORY: Movie Titles / LETTER: T"_ (Pop culture, broad, many answers: Titanic, Toy Story, The Matrix, etc.)

## Game 14: Over/Under - Card Generation Guide

### What the AI is Generating:

**Numerical Questions About Someone** - Questions that have a specific number answer, allowing the group to bet on whether the real number is higher or lower than their guess.

### The Formula:

`"[QUESTION ABOUT THE SUBJECT THAT HAS A NUMERICAL ANSWER]"`

### The Psychology:

This game combines **social judgment** and **gambling**. The best questions are:

1.  **Verifiable** (the Subject can prove the answer).
2.  **Surprising** (the answer is often higher or lower than expected).
3.  **Revealing** (the answer says something about the Subject's personality or habits).

### Card Categories:

#### **Digital Dirt (Phone Checks):**

Questions that require the Subject to check their phone for an exact number.

-   _"Number of unread emails."_
-   _"Number of alarms currently set."_
-   _"Screen time usage for yesterday (in minutes)."_
-   _"Number of photos in your Camera Roll."_
-   _"Number of contacts in your phone."_

**Why it works:** These are instantly verifiable. The Subject opens their phone, and the truth is revealed.

#### **The Past (History):**

Questions about the Subject's personal history or life experiences.

-   _"Body count (sexual partners)."_ _(High risk, high reward)_
-   _"Age you had your first kiss."_
-   _"Number of times you've been arrested or detained."_
-   _"Number of jobs you've had in your life."_
-   _"Number of countries you've visited."_

**Why it works:** These questions are personal and often surprising. The group thinks they know the Subject, but they're usually wrong.

#### **The Hypothetical (Capabilities):**

Questions that test the Subject's abilities or knowledge in real-time.

-   _"How many push-ups can you do right now?"_ (Subject must perform)
-   _"How much cash is in your wallet right now?"_
-   _"How many state capitals can you name in 60 seconds?"_
-   _"How many digits of pi can you recite?"_

**Why it works:** These questions are testable on the spot. The Subject can't lie‚Äîthey have to prove it.

#### **The Social Media:**

Questions about the Subject's online presence and activity.

-   _"Number of Instagram followers."_
-   _"Number of TikTok videos you've posted."_
-   _"Number of dating app matches (if single)."_
-   _"Number of Facebook friends."_

**Why it works:** Social media numbers are public (or semi-public), so the group can verify. They also reveal how "popular" or "active" the Subject is online.

#### **The Habits:**

Questions about the Subject's daily routines or behaviors.

-   _"Number of cups of coffee you drink per day."_
-   _"Number of hours you sleep per night (on average)."_
-   _"Number of times you've gone to the gym this month."_
-   _"Number of books you've read this year."_

**Why it works:** These questions reveal lifestyle choices. The group will judge the Subject based on the answer.

#### **The "Embarrassing Truth":**

Questions that are mildly embarrassing but funny.

-   _"Number of times you've watched your favorite movie."_
-   _"Number of selfies on your phone."_
-   _"Number of times you've Googled yourself."_
-   _"Number of plants you've killed."_

**Why it works:** These questions are lighthearted but revealing. The Subject's answer will either confirm or shatter the group's assumptions.

### Generation Rules:

-   **Make it verifiable.** "How many times have you lied?" is impossible to verify. "How many unread texts do you have?" is instant.
-   **Avoid questions that are too personal.** "How much money do you make?" can create genuine discomfort.
-   **Test for surprise factor.** If the answer is always predictable, the betting isn't fun.
-   **Balance high and low numbers.** "Number of hairs on your head" is too high. "Number of siblings" is too low (and boring).

### The "Line-Setting" Strategy:

The group must agree on a "Line" (the predicted number). A good Line:

1.  **Splits the room 50/50.** Half the group bets Over, half bets Under.
2.  **Is based on evidence.** "He posts on Instagram daily, so let's set the line at 500 followers."
3.  **Allows for debate.** "Wait, does he _really_ have 500 followers? I think it's lower."

### Bad Examples (and Why):

-   ‚ùå _"Number of thoughts you've had today."_ (Impossible to verify)
-   ‚ùå _"Number of siblings."_ (Too low, no betting tension)
-   ‚ùå _"Your annual salary."_ (Too personal, kills the vibe)

### Good Examples (and Why):

-   ‚úÖ _"Number of unread text messages."_ (Instantly verifiable, often surprising)
-   ‚úÖ _"Screen time usage yesterday (in minutes)."_ (Reveals phone addiction, group can debate)
-   ‚úÖ _"Number of exes you still follow on Instagram."_ (Mildly embarrassing, verifiable, funny)

## Conclusion: The Art of the Perfect Card

Generating cards for party games is not about complexity‚Äîit's about **emotional leverage**. The best cards:

1.  **Are instantly understandable** (Drunk Person Test).
2.  **Create social tension** (judgment, competition, embarrassment).
3.  **Allow for creativity** (multiple valid answers or interpretations).
4.  **Are universally relatable** (avoid niche references).
5.  **Balance humor and stakes** (funny, not hurtful).

When in doubt, ask yourself:

-   **Would I want to answer this card in front of my friends?**
-   **Would this card make me laugh or make me leave the room?**
-   **Can someone who's three drinks in understand this immediately?**

If the answer to all three is "yes," you've created a perfect card.

Now go forth and generate chaos.
